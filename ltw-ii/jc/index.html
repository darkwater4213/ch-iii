Then Falls Caesar!

Marcus Junius Brutus was in a predicament. His best friend, Gaius Julius Caesar, had risen to power. He had assumed command of the Roman legions in Gaul and the surrounding areas, leading them to massive strategic victories against the various tribes in those areas. He then returned to Rome in Triumph. He had hoped hs friends, Cassius and Brutus, would join him in celebrating Rome's victory. But neither quite accepted. Brutus and Cassius both knew that the only victory Caesar had won was over other Romans. Brutus saw a failure in the system — a fault in the stars. But Cassius saw an opportunity. And he took that opportunity to strike at Caesar. But he could not do it alone. He needed Brutus. And Brutus saw the logic in Cassius's words — it was to be the death of him. Not of Brutus, but of Caesar... And finally Justice might be served. Honor might prevail. Justice for the Roman people. Honor of Caesar in death. But what of Justice for Caesar — what of Brutus's Honor? Surely there must be recompense. He had killed. And Caesar's last words would haunt him for the rest of his life…

Fist of all, indeed, above all, Caesar and Brutus were very fast friends. Just below that is the Roman ideal of Honor, of which both Caesar and Brutus had a very firm grasp and adhered to most strictly. This meant that Brutus would not kill without direct intent. He wished to spare Rome — spare Rome the yoke of Caesar's rule, spare them the burden of Caesar as their king. He would not wish such agony on her; Caesar could not be permitted to ascend the throne. He made this clear to Cassius and the rest. (II.i 10-34)

But Brutus's actions call into question his judgement, do they not? — even his sanity. His actions as the arc unfolds devolve rapidly, becoming ever more erratic, even twisted and evil. Except that this is simply not the case. Brutus demanded that the conspirators maintain the highest standard of Honor to the very end, even if it meant letting their forces go on furlough1.

And Brutus did not act alone. Cassius, Casca, Cinna, Decius, Metellus, and Trebonius all were part of the plot to kill Caesar. Brutus was the only conspirator with pure intentions — and the only one who realized that Caesar's death was not necessary at the outset. But things change. Plans change. And even the greatest must fall. The blame should not — indeed, it cannot fall to Brutus1. (III.ii)

But didn't Brutus betray his friend? He was trusted by Caesar implicitly and utterly. Caesar's last words were those of dismay and betrayal, were they not? "Et tu, Brute, — then fall Caesar!" But — there is no question. It is a statement. And you, Brutus. If Brutus says, "Thus Caesar falls," — then thus falls Caesar! The Roman ideal of Honor did not call for complaints or whining. When your friend betrays you, you take it like the Roman man you are1.

He was not expressing bitterness or betrayal or dismay or pleading for mercy or help; he was very clearly assenting Brutus's decision. Caesar was recognizing Brutus's decision as for the greater cause of Rome, that is to say, Caesar's last words were a pardon unto Brutus. The great Roman general himself pardoned Brutus from any consequences resulting from his actions1. (III.i 80)

Brutus is guilty, it is true. A pardon cannot be accepted without an admission of guilt. But Brutus's guilt was never in question: he clearly killed his fellow. But Caesar very clearly realized in his last moments that Brutus's actions were indeed Honorable — and to the greater glory of Rome, not Brutus. He noted this and, in the same breath, forgave and pardoned his own killer. Anyone who calls into question Brutus's Honor is clearly in the wrong simply by the words of the only judge who possibly could have the grounds on which to judge Brutus's actions.
